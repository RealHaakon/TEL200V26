\section{Abstract}
% ============================================================
This report concerns the design and implementation of a simulated quadruped
walking robot. The project covers three main topics: motion planning for
coordinated leg movement, path planning for navigation, and
localization and mapping based on real lidar data. Together, these form the
core components of an autonomous robotic system.

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================
\subsection{Motion Planning}

A quadruped walking robot requires coordinated control of four legs, each
kinematically equivalent to a three-joint serial-link manipulator. Gait
generation ensures static stability by keeping at least three feet in contact
with the ground at all times, dividing each leg's motion into a stance phase
and a reset phase. The resulting crawl gait is achieved by assigning each leg
a phase-shifted version of the same cyclic foot trajectory.

\subsection{Path Planning}

For a robot to navigate an environment, it must plan a collision-free path from
a start position to a goal. The Probabilistic Roadmap (PRM) method addresses
this by constructing a sparse graph of obstacle-free paths through the free
space during an offline planning phase. Once built, the roadmap can be reused
to answer any start-to-goal query efficiently, making PRM well suited to
large-scale and high-dimensional planning problems.

\subsection{Localization and Mapping}

Autonomous navigation requires a robot to know both its own position
(localization) and the structure of its environment (mapping). These two
problems are interdependent: a map is needed for localization, and a pose
estimate is needed for mapping. For the particular problem using the real lidar data over Killian court,
an evaluation between PRM and A* search algorithm is made

\section{Method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Motion Planning}

A robot leg is kinematically equivalent to a robot arm. For a statically stable
quadruped, a three-joint serial-link manipulator is sufficient, since the foot
makes point contact with the ground and orientation is not critical
\cite{Corke2023}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=3cm]{images/Leg.png}
    \caption{Illustration of frame and axis rotations for the simple leg
    \cite{Corke2023}}
\end{figure}

The coordinate frame is defined with the $x$-axis pointing forward, the
$z$-axis upward, and the $y$-axis to the left. The leg link lengths are
$L_1 = L_2 = -100\,\text{mm}$. The hip has two degrees of freedom: rotation
about the $z$-axis (forward/backward) and rotation about the $x$-axis
(up/down). The knee is offset by $L_1$ in the $y$-direction and rotates about
the $x$-axis, while the foot is offset by $L_2$ in the $z$-direction. In ETS
notation:

\begin{equation}
    \texttt{leg} = R_z \cdot R_x \cdot T_y(L_1) \cdot R_x \cdot T_z(L_2)
\end{equation}

At zero joint angles, forward kinematics yields a foot position of
$(0,\ -0.1,\ -0.1)\,\text{m}$, confirming the design.

During the \textit{stance phase}, the foot remains in contact with the ground
50\,mm below the hip, moving from 50\,mm forward to 50\,mm behind the hip to
propel the body forward. During the \textit{reset phase}, the foot lifts to
20\,mm below the hip, swings forward, and is placed back on the ground. To
maintain static stability, at least three feet must remain on the ground at all
times, so each leg spends 75\% of the cycle in stance and 25\% resetting.
This coordinated pattern is known as a \textit{crawl gait} (or \textit{wave
gait}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=3.5cm]{images/TrajectoryAndMotion.png}
    \caption{Trajectory of a single foot and motion of each leg
    \cite{Corke2023}}
\end{figure}

The foot path is defined by five via points forming a closed cycle:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{p}_{\text{via}} =
    \begin{bmatrix}
        x_f & y & z_d \\
        x_b & y & z_d \\
        x_b & y & z_u \\
        x_f & y & z_u \\
        x_f & y & z_d
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

where $x_f = 50\,\text{mm}$, $x_b = -50\,\text{mm}$, $y = -50\,\text{mm}$,
$z_u = -20\,\text{mm}$, and $z_d = -50\,\text{mm}$. The path is sampled at
100\,Hz using \texttt{mstraj} with segment times
$[3,\ 0.25,\ 0.5,\ 0.25]\,\text{s}$, giving a 4\,s cycle of 400 points.
Joint angle trajectories are obtained by numerical inverse kinematics, solving
only for end-effector translation:

\begin{equation}
    \texttt{mask} = [1,\ 1,\ 1,\ 0,\ 0,\ 0]
\end{equation}

The robot body has width $W = 100\,\text{mm}$ and length $L = 200\,\text{mm}$.
Four leg instances are placed at the body corners using base transforms. Legs
on the left side (legs 2 and 3) are rotated by $\pi$ about the $z$-axis so
they point outward from the body. Each leg runs the same cyclic trajectory with
a phase offset of one quarter of the cycle (100 points), achieved via modulo
indexing:

\begin{equation}
    k' = (k + \phi) \mod N
\end{equation}

where $k$ is the current time step, $\phi \in \{0, 100, 200, 300\}$ is the
phase offset per leg, and $N = 400$ is the total cycle length. For legs on the
right-hand side, the sign of the first joint angle is flipped to mirror the
motion symmetrically.

For global positioning the robot is modelled as \textit{unicycle}, a common kinematic abstraction
for mobile robots moving on a planar surface. The robot pose is fully
described by three states:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_v = (x,\, y,\, \theta)^T
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[height=2.5cm]{images/Unicycle-model-of-a-wheeled-mobile-robot.png}
    \caption{Illustration of pose using unicycle model
    \cite{Unicycle_model}}
\end{figure}

where $x$ and $y$ are the position coordinates in the global reference frame
and $\theta$ is the heading angle relative to the $x$-axis.

\subsection{Motion Primitives}

The global motion of the robot is defined by two discrete motion primitives for
forward translation of 10cm, and in-place rotation of 1\degree. Each primitive
corresponds to one full gait cycle. A forward cycle produces a 10cm 
displacement in the current heading direction, while a rotation cycle changes
 the orientation by 1\degree. The robot pose is updated using the unicycle model.

\begin{equation}
x_{k+1} = x_k + \Delta s \cos\theta_k,\qquad
y_{k+1} = y_k + \Delta s \sin\theta_k,\qquad
\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k + \Delta\theta
\end{equation}



Utilizing these motion primitives, we can achieve planar motion
by combining forward and rotational steps. However since both
translation and rotation are reduced to certain numbers, some loss of precision occurs.

Forward motion is performed in steps of 100\,mm. This means the robot
cannot move an exact distance smaller than 100\,mm. When approaching a
waypoint, the maximum positional overshoot is therefore limited to
approximately 50\,mm (half a step). Similarly, rotational motion is
limited to integer degrees, making it impossible to represent
non-integer heading angles exactly.

Over longer paths, these small discretization effects may accumulate and
cause deviation from the ideal continuous trajectory. To reduce this
effect, the robot continuously tracks its position relative to the next
node in the PRM path. Any small translational or angular overshoot is
naturally corrected when aligning toward the following node.

Although this approach reduces mobility resolution, the resulting error
remains bounded and acceptable for the scale of the simulated
environment.

\subsection{Path Planning}

Robot path planning is divided into two phases: a \textit{planning phase},
which analyses the map and constructs an obstacle-free network, and a
\textit{query phase}, which finds a path between a given start and goal using
that network. Roadmap methods decouple these phases, so the roadmap is computed
once and reused for any start and goal combination \cite{Corke2023}.

The Probabilistic Roadmap (PRM) method builds the roadmap by randomly sampling
free-space vertices and connecting them with obstacle-free straight-line edges,
forming an embedded graph. With sufficiently many vertices, the graph spans the
entire free space. The connectivity depends on the number of sampled points;
too few vertices may result in disjoint components, preventing valid paths from
being found.

Once the roadmap is constructed, the query phase finds a path by connecting the
start position to the nearest roadmap vertex, traversing the graph via a
minimum-distance route, and exiting at the vertex closest to the goal. The
path is represented as an ordered list of via points:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{p} = \{\mathbf{p}_0,\ \mathbf{p}_1,\ \dots,\ \mathbf{p}_n\}
\end{equation}

Let the robot pose be $(x_r, y_r, \theta_r)$ and the next waypoint
$(x_t, y_t)$. The required walking and turning cycles are computed as

\begin{equation}
d = \sqrt{(x_t - x_r)^2 + (y_t - y_r)^2},
\qquad
N_{\text{walk}} = \mathrm{round}\!\left(\frac{d}{100\,\text{mm}}\right),
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\Delta\theta =
\left((\mathrm{atan2}(y_t - y_r, x_t - x_r) - \theta_r + \pi)
\bmod 2\pi\right) - \pi,
\qquad
N_{\text{turn}} =
\mathrm{round}\!\left(\frac{\Delta\theta}{1^\circ}\right).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Localization and Mapping}

Robot navigation requires two fundamental capabilities: knowing where the robot
is (localization) and knowing what the environment looks like (mapping). These
problems are deeply interdependent --- a map is needed for accurate
localization, while a reliable pose estimate is needed to build a map
\cite{Corke2023}.

With the lidar data provided by the robot, we can map the area onto an occupancy grid in python
using the scanmap() method.
The occupancy grid is represented as a grid of cells, where each cell has a value indicating
its occupancy. The way this value is calculated is by first going through the rays and using the Bresenham line-drawing
algorithm to find the cells each ray passes through. The cell at the end of its path has its value reduced by one, and the rest
of the cells on the path has their respective values increased by one. Through this incrementation, each cell that has rays pass through
them ends up with a value indicating how occupied it is.
In the scanmap() method, it then goes on to clamp the values to be either $-1$, $0$ or $1$. If
the cell's value is negative, it is assumed to be occupied, and its final value is $-1$. If
the cell's value is zero, it is decided to be unknown, and the value stays at zero. And finally,
if the cell's value is positive, it is assumed to be free space, and the value is set to $1$.
\\\\
To modify the scanmap() method to arrive at a binary occupancy grid, where if more than $M$
lidar scans confirm a cell is free, the cell has value $0$, otherwise $1$, a few steps were taken:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The decrementation of cells needs to be removed, as the task description states that a cell is free if more than $M$
    rays pass through it, not if at least $M$ more rays pass through than don't.
    \item In the original implementation it only goes through $20\%$ of the rays. This needs to be modified to go
    through all rays.
    \item The final step in the scanmap() method needs to be modified for a threshold of $M$ rather than
    $0$. The clamp in the final step also needs to be modified to clamp to $0$ and $1$ to make it binary.
    And the final step also needs to be modified to return a binary occupancy grid.
\end{itemize}

intended area, npoints is set rather high in order to make proper coverage of the intended area more probable.
\\\\
An alternative path planning algorithm that can be more efficient than PRM is $A^*$. The way this works is
you store three essential values for each cell in the grid:
\begin{align*}
    g(n) &\rightarrow \text{The calculated cost from start to here} \\
    h(n) &\rightarrow \text{The estimated cost from here to goal} \\
    f(n) &\rightarrow \text{The sum of $g$ and $h$, the total estimated cost of path}
\end{align*}
You sort these into two different categories: Open, which are yet to be evaluated, and closed, which have
been evaluated. You evaluate the neighbour cells of the current cell in this way until you've either reached
the goal, and thus have found the cheapest path, or until the open category is empty, in which case

\section{Results}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Motion Planning}
The implemented crawl gait produced stable forward motion in the
simulation. At all times, at least three legs remained in contact with
the ground, ensuring static stability. No tipping or oscillatory
instabilities were observed during straight-line walking.

Each gait cycle resulted in an approximate forward displacement of
100\,mm, as intended by the motion primitive design. Small deviations
from the ideal displacement were observed due to numerical inverse
kinematics and floating-point rounding, but these errors remained
negligible over short distances. The turning was also affected by
floating point precision errors, however the angular error did not cause instability.

When combining rotation and translation primitives, the robot was able
to approximate arbitrary planar trajectories. Although the motion is
piecewise discrete rather than continuous, the resulting path closely
resembled the desired trajectory for the scale of the simulated
environment.

Overall, the motion primitive framework provided predictable and stable
locomotion, with bounded discretization error that was acceptable for
subsequent path-following tasks.


\subsection{Path Planning}

The PRM graph contains a parameter specifying the number of nodes in the
roadmap. The objective is to determine a suitable node count for the given
house map. If too few nodes are used, the graph may contain disconnected
subgraphs (see Fig.~\ref{fig:prm_density}), causing large parts of the
environment to become unreachable. On the other hand, using too many nodes
increases computational cost and reduces performance.

To determine a suitable value, one could use an analytical iterative
approach. However, for this project an empirical approach was chosen,
as it is sufficient for evaluating performance in a fixed environment.

To execute a planned path, the robot is first placed at the start
coordinate using the custom \texttt{GOTO(x, y)} method in the
\texttt{WalkingRobot} class. The robot continuously evaluates its own pose
relative to the next waypoint in the path. This compensates for the
accumulated discretization error introduced by the motion primitives
described in Section~3.2.

The robot executes the primitives sequentially: it first performs the
required number of turning cycles, then executes the walking cycles.
This process is repeated for each waypoint until the final node in the
path is reached.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Motion Planning and Path Following}

The implemented leg kinematics produced stable motion in simulation. While the
model behaves consistently in the simulated environment, it is likely
that a physical implementation would require more refined modeling,
better dynamics handling, and feedback control to achieve smooth and
realistic motion.

A major limitation of the system is the use of only two motion
primitives: 100\,mm forward translation and $1^\circ$ in-place rotation.
Although this simplifies control, it significantly limits motion
resolution. The robot cannot perform fine adjustments efficiently.
Turning is slow because large rotations require many
$1^\circ$ cycles. A more flexible primitive set (e.g., $30^\circ$,
$10^\circ$, $5^\circ$, and $1^\circ$ rotations) would improve both
speed and performance.

During path execution, the robot was generally able to follow the
planned paths. However, small overshoot and undershoot effects were
observed due to discretization of motion. These errors remained bounded
and were partially corrected when the robot aligned toward the next
waypoint.

The generated PRM paths were valid but not always smooth. Since the
robot executes a turn-then-walk strategy at each waypoint, the overall
motion becomes piecewise and slow. Additionally, the PRM struggles in narrow rooms and near
corners, where random sampling may fail to provide sufficient coverage.
As a result, some regions close to walls are difficult or impossible
to reach. However as a side bonus this can make up for the absence of collision detection,
by making the robot less likely to walk near walls and corners.

In general the system is modular and well structured.
The separation between gait generation, motion primitives, and path
planning makes it straightforward to replace the PRM algorithm or
extend the motion primitive set. Despite its simplifications, the robot
successfully completed all required tasks in the assignment.

\subsection{Localization and mapping}
From Fig.~\ref{fig:prmKillian}, the binary occupancy grid produced by the
modified scanmap() method captures the main structure of the Killian hallways.
Walls are clearly defined, and the free space inside corridors is visible.
This indicates that the lidar processing and thresholding approach works
as intended for generating a usable map representation.
\\\\
From the PRM map attempts it can also be observed that even with a huge amount
of nodes, the PRM is still unable to provide sufficient connectivity throughout 
the Killian hallways. This drastically increases the chance of an error when attempting to 
plan a path between two points in the environment. This is because tight corridors and
sharp turns require more points concentrated around them in order to establish connectivity.
Therefore, the amount of points needs to be quite large for the probability of connection to be
at a reliable level. 
\\\\
The $A^*$ algorithm on the other hand is able to cover the entire grid, and with it's
best first search approach is likely to find the best path quite quickly. Because of the tight
hallways and sharp turns of the Killian court, the $A^*$ algorithm is therefore more efficient
to consider.
